I Lost a Week. The System Never Noticed.

What xylazine did to me, why the treatment system had no framework for it, and what has to change.

I want to tell you what happened to me during withdrawal. Not as a cautionary tale. Not as a confession. I want to tell you because I spent time after the fact trying to understand it — pharmacologically, systematically — and when I did, the picture that came together wasn't about personal failure.

It was about a drug supply that changed, a body that responded the way bodies do, and a system that had no framework for any of it.

I lost approximately a week of my life. I woke up in a hospital, handcuffed to a gurney, with no clear memory of how I got there or what had happened in between. People I knew couldn't believe I had survived the circumstances. I had been in a state of psychosis and delirium for days — moving through corrections facilities, escalating, deteriorating — and at no point had anyone identified what was medically happening to me.

📄 A note on the records
I've requested my institutional and medical records through FOIA. When they arrive, I'll publish them separately and link back here. What's written now is from memory, from research, and from the understanding I've built after the fact.

The Arc

Day one: I still knew where I was

I arrived into custody approximately 24 hours after my last use. I was physically sick in the way that anyone withdrawing from opioids is sick — unable to eat, in pain, exhausted. But I was cognitively present. I could answer questions. I could follow instructions. I understood my situation and I could think about it clearly.

That clarity is worth noting, because it disappeared. And the disappearance of it was not gradual and obvious. It was something that crept in from the edges while I was still, from the inside, convinced I was thinking normally.

Day two: the paranoia

By the second day, I became convinced that someone on my range had informed on me to police. This was not a vague unease. It was a certainty. I had specific reasons I could articulate. The logic felt sound. What I did not have access to, from inside that state, was any perspective on whether the logic was actually connected to reality.

I understand now what was happening physiologically. The adrenergic rebound was building. Norepinephrine was flooding regions of the brain associated with threat detection. The amygdala and related structures, which are heavily innervated by noradrenergic pathways, were essentially running in overdrive. Paranoia in that state is not a psychological quirk. It is a direct product of a nervous system that has been chemically reconfigured to read everything as threat.

I had one moment of doubt — a flicker of recognition that I might be wrong. I remember it. But I was already in motion, already committed, already in circumstances where backing down felt more dangerous than going forward. I lost the thread of that doubt almost immediately.

The thing about losing capacity is that you don't know you've lost it.
From the inside, everything still feels like reasoning. It just isn't.

The escalation and what followed

What followed was a series of escalating situations that I am not going to describe in detail here, partly because the detail matters less than what it illustrates, and partly because I do not want this to read as something it isn't. I was not in control of what was happening. I was not making strategic decisions. I was a person in an acute neurological crisis, moving through an environment that was responding to my behaviour as a conduct problem without any awareness that it was a medical one.

I was transferred. I received methadone at one point — a nurse was kind to me, told me to lie down and that I was going to be okay. That was the only clinical intervention I received during that window. It addressed the opioid component of what was happening to me. It did not touch the adrenergic crisis.

I remember thinking, somewhere in those days, that I was going to treatment. That the chaos around me was somehow leading somewhere purposeful. I have no idea now where that thought came from. It may have been the mind trying to construct a narrative that made sense of an experience that didn't.

Waking up

I woke up in the Ottawa General Hospital. I was handcuffed and shackled to a gurney. I did not know how much time had passed. I did not know, initially, how I had gotten there or what had happened.

It had been approximately a week.

I had been admitted in a state of delirium. I had apparently been seriously confused and non-functional for several days. People who knew me were surprised I came back to the range I was eventually returned to — I had no memory of which range I should have been on. I was eventually transferred to isolation, which is where I finally began to sleep and slowly recover some sense of what had happened.

I was not fully myself when I was discharged from the hospital and returned. I was approximately 85 percent functional by my own estimate. No one assessed that. No one asked. I was returned to the institution and the institution asked me which range I wanted, and I told them the wrong one, and it was only the intervention of a staff member who recognized the danger that prevented a situation that could have been very serious.

A week of my life. A hospital admission. Multiple staff interventions. Significant institutional resources.

None of it moved me one step closer to treatment. I came out of it more destabilized than I went in.

The Gap

What the System Saw, and What It Missed

I want to be careful here not to blame individual people. The nurse who brought me methadone was genuinely kind. The staff member who recognized I was going to the wrong range may have prevented something much worse. People were doing their jobs.

The failure was not individual. It was structural. At no point in my movement through those systems was there a framework for identifying what was actually happening to me: a person in adrenergic rebound from xylazine exposure, layered on top of opioid withdrawal, moving through a setting that had no clinical tools to recognize or respond to either.

This is what the continuum of care looks like for someone with fentanyl-xylazine dependence in Canada today, when things go wrong:

Stage What the system sees What is actually happening
Intake Sick, compliant, manageable Opioid withdrawal beginning, adrenergic rebound building
Day 2–3 Agitated, difficult, behavioural problem Adrenergic peak: paranoia, threat hypervigilance, loss of cognitive capacity
Escalation Conduct issue, safety concern Person in neurological crisis, no longer able to reason or self-advocate
Hospital Delirium, psychiatric presentation Withdrawal-driven crisis, arriving late and in acute state
Discharge Medically cleared, return to facility Still not connected to treatment, no bridge to care, cycle continues

The critical issue is consent and capacity. Treatment systems, ethically and legally, require a person to be able to consent to care. They require self-advocacy — the ability to say "I need help" and engage with what is being offered. Adrenergic rebound and delirium are precisely the states in which that capacity is destroyed. The person most in need of treatment is, at the peak of this withdrawal pattern, the least able to ask for it or engage with it.

And social or non-medical detox settings — the level of care most people actually access, because medical detox beds are limited and waitlisted — have no tools to manage what is happening. They cannot prescribe the α2-agonist medications that address adrenergic rebound. They cannot monitor blood pressure. They cannot provide the clinical environment that prevents the deterioration I experienced. So people leave. Not because they gave up on getting clean. Because their nervous system is in crisis and relief is available and the setting cannot do anything to help them through the peak.

They leave, they use, and the cycle continues. Or they deteriorate in custody, or in a shelter, or on the street, and eventually arrive at a hospital or a jail in a state that is far more expensive and far more dangerous than an adequately staffed medical detox bed would have been.

What Needs to Change

The System Needs to Catch Up

I am not a policy analyst. But I have lived inside the gap between what exists and what is needed, and I think that perspective has value.

What detox means has to be redefined

Social withdrawal management — a safe place with support but no medical capacity — was built for a different drug supply. It was adequate for heroin-era withdrawal, which is primarily opioid-driven and rarely medically dangerous on its own. It is not adequate for fentanyl-xylazine withdrawal. Adequate detox for the current supply requires physician oversight, monitoring, and the ability to prescribe medications that address the adrenergic system. That is a medical detox. The definition needs to move, and the funding needs to follow it.

The consent and capacity problem needs to be named

If someone is in delirium, they cannot consent to treatment. If someone is in the grip of adrenergic rebound and paranoia, their cognitive capacity is impaired in ways that are neurologically specific and pharmacologically explainable. The systems around them — corrections, hospital, detox — need frameworks for recognizing this and responding to it medically rather than behaviourally.

Corrections needs clinical literacy on withdrawal

Jail intake processes are not designed to identify adrenergic rebound. They are designed to process people. A person arriving sick from withdrawal, who is cognitively present on day one and deteriorating by day three, needs a clinical response, not a conduct response. The two things look similar from the outside. They have completely different causes and completely different solutions.

The bridge to treatment has to exist

I came out of the hospital and went back to an institution. No one connected me to treatment. No one created a plan. The crisis consumed enormous resources — hospital admission, transport, staff interventions, segregation — and produced nothing in terms of care continuity. That is a design failure. The moment of crisis, however chaotic, should be the entry point to something. Right now, for most people, it is not.

A Note on the FOIA Request

I have requested my institutional and medical records through the Freedom of Information process. When those documents arrive, I will publish them in a separate post and link back to this one. I want to see what the system recorded about that week — what language was used, what was documented, what was not. I think the gap between what the records will show and what I have described here will itself be informative.

If you have had a similar experience — withdrawal that deteriorated into something your care setting could not manage, capacity you lost and could not recover in time to help yourself — I would like to hear from you. Not for any formal purpose. Just because these stories tend to stay private, and they should not.

The drug supply changed. The system did not.
And somewhere in that gap, people are losing weeks of their lives, or more than that.
