Ontario's $3.8B Problem — Who Profits When You Don't Recover?

Ontario spent $3.8 billion on addiction treatment. The system is still broken. Here's where the money went — and why it didn't reach the people who needed it most.

Josh Pearsall APRIL 2026  ·  Recovery Roofer

The first time I went to detox, I believed them. That's the thing nobody talks about — how much hope there is at the start. Your parents believe them too. They'll pay anything, sign anything, drive anywhere. Nobody hands you a handbook. Nobody tells you what the system actually is or who's actually getting paid. They just tell you this is the answer.

How We Got Here

Ontario's addiction crisis didn't appear overnight. For two decades the landscape shifted underneath people's feet while policy tried to catch up — and mostly missed.

In the early days, MAT — Medication Assisted Treatment — was the last resort. You had to prove chronic homelessness, HIV, Hepatitis C, or serious criminal history just to qualify. Only specific doctors were authorized under Health Canada to prescribe it. It was hard to access by design.

Then OxyContin happened. And when the reformulated OxyNeo hit the market — harder to crush, harder to abuse — it didn't solve the problem. It just pushed people toward heroin, and eventually fentanyl. The government's response was a knee-jerk expansion of MAT. Overnight it went from last resort to first line of defence. Fee-for-service clinics opened. Doctors who could bill for it, did. Two urine tests a week. Mandatory counselling. Supplemental medications — senna, clonidine, whatever else they could prescribe. Every touchpoint a billable moment.

Nobody got paid when you got better. They got paid when you showed up.

The Number

In 2020 Ontario announced $3.8 billion for mental health and addictions over ten years. That's three thousand million dollars and change. When I heard that number my first thought was — where?

Peer support workers make $20 an hour. Detox beds have waitlists. People are still navigating four different intake forms to access the same broken system they were in six months ago. $3.8 billion sounds like progress. It's supposed to. That's what it's designed to do.

The money went to hospitals. To CAMH. To established mental health organizations that already had infrastructure, lobbyists, and government relationships. To new HART Hubs — Homelessness and Addiction Recovery Treatment — a rebranding of community care that shifted funding away from consumption sites toward treatment-focused models. It went to digital tools and virtual care platforms. To nonprofit annual reports with professional layouts and mission statements. What it didn't go to — not enough of it anyway — was the person sitting in detox for the third time wondering why nothing is working.

What It Didn't Fix

They call it Medication Assisted Treatment. What they don't tell you is that for many people, the medication becomes the treatment — indefinitely. Methadone felt like liquid handcuffs. The same thing they promise will give you your life back is something you may never get off.

Clinics with strange hours. Can't travel without prior approval. Show up every day, drink it at the window, get processed and leave. The community has a word for it. Methadonian. Junkie with a prescription. The stigma doesn't go away — it just gets a government stamp on it.

Nobody warned me about the side effects either. Testosterone levels tank in men — widely underreported. Your teeth deteriorate. These aren't rare complications, they're common ones. They just don't make it into the brochure.

And when you do get stable — when you're actually doing better — the system starts charging you. Carries aren't covered once you're deemed functional enough to pay. Insulin is covered. SSRIs are covered. But methadone carries? That's on you. It's a class thing. That's the only honest way to say it.

Too Many Chiefs

Ontario closed nine supervised consumption sites and replaced them with HART Hubs. The government called it a shift toward recovery. Critics called it a political decision dressed up as a health one. A year in, many hubs are still only partially operational. Demand far exceeds capacity and promised services are still being rolled out.

In Hamilton, overdoses increased after the consumption site closed. Paramedics responded to a record number of suspected overdose calls in January. The hub's own director said it is not a direct replacement for what was lost.

In Ottawa, I know this street level. The same people I used to use with hang out in front of the HART Hub. Same plot of land. Different sign.

We stopped giving people clean gear. Now we'll pay for the Hep C treatment later. The math doesn't work — unless you're the one billing for the treatment.

And the housing piece — the part that sounds most promising on paper — misses something nobody in the boardroom seems to understand. Putting someone from active addiction into a supportive housing unit doesn't automatically give them the skills to live independently. You can end up isolated in a room, not knowing how to do dishes, with a dealer who figured out your address before your caseworker did. That's not recovery. That's warehousing.

Nobody Gets Paid When You Recover

Here's the thing nobody wants to say out loud. The system isn't broken. It's working exactly as designed — for the people getting paid to run it.

Methadone clinics bill per visit. Urine screens are billable. Counselling sessions are billable. Intake appointments, assessments, transition planning — all billable. The moment you recover and stop showing up, the revenue stops. There is no billing code for success.

Front line staff aren't the problem — most of them got into this work because they care. The problem is above them. It's in the policy, the funding models, the incentive structures built into the system from the top down. Too many organizations protecting their piece of the budget. Too many ministries with overlapping mandates. Too many chiefs, not enough people actually on the ground doing the work.

Every stop on that loop is someone's revenue stream.

It Only Needs to Work Once

Nobody gets it until it's them. Your friends, your colleagues, your family — they all know exactly what they would have done differently. It's easy to have the answer when it's not your kid, not your partner, not you at 3am making a decision you can't take back.

The system will frustrate you. It will repeat itself. It will hand you paperwork when you need a bed, and a bed when you need skills, and skills when you need someone to just show up.

But here's what I know after two decades — street living, federal and provincial time, psychosis, seizures on rooftops, the whole ride: it only needs to work once.

So if you're reading this for someone you love — don't give up on the click. Push for better information. Ask where the money goes. Demand more than a waiting list and a pamphlet.

And if you're reading this because it's you — I've got a son now. A job. I taught myself to type, learned hardware and software, built something from nothing. The system didn't do that. I did. But someone showed me it was possible.

That's all this is.
