Internal Motivation: The Fuel That Keeps You Rebuilding When Everything Else Is Gone

When I walked out of custody in my mid-thirties, I had nothing. No ID, no bank account, a decade of active addiction, and a head full of prison rules that didn’t work on the outside. The first few weeks the “Motivation Window” was wide open — fear, shame, and the shock of freedom pushed me hard. But windows close. Court dates end, family pressure eases, and the daily grind of shelters, waitlists, and proving yourself at a new job sets in That’s when internal motivation became my real lifeline

Josh Pearsall March 2026  ·  Recovery Roofer

That’s when internal motivation became my real lifeline.

Internal motivation (sometimes called intrinsic motivation) is the drive that comes from inside you, not from outside rewards or punishments. It’s the quiet voice that says “I want to do this because it matters to me” instead of “I have to do this or else…”

Psychologists break it down with Self-Determination Theory into three basic needs that keep the fire burning:

  • Autonomy – Feeling like you’re choosing your path, even when options feel limited.
  • Competence – Getting better and feeling effective at something.
  • Relatedness – Feeling connected to something bigger than yourself.

I’ve had to rebuild more than once. Each time the external stuff (money, approval, fear) got stripped away, I leaned harder on these three things. But I also learned something else from my earlier life that still helps on the really shitty days: you can recycle your own shit — anger, pain, and negative emotions — and turn it into short-term energy if you harness it right.

Back in high school and junior days, I wrestled and played football. Those sports beat the hell out of me — literally. I got pinned, tackled, knocked down, screamed at by coaches, and embarrassed in front of teammates more times than I can count. The adversity was constant. But I figured out early that anger could be fuel. When I was down 8-2 in a match or getting my ass handed to me on the line, I didn’t just get mad — I used it. That hot rush in my chest became extra power in my hips for a takedown or drive in the trenches. It wasn’t pretty, but it got me through when my body wanted to quit.

I still do the same thing today in recovery and on the roof.

When a tough day hits — sore back from roofing, craving sneaking up, or just that heavy “why the fuck am I doing this again?” feeling — I don’t try to stuff it down or fake positivity. I recycle it. I let myself sit with the anger or the memory of how bad it used to be. I ruminate on purpose: the nights in custody, the bridges I burned, the times I let my family down, the version of me that was completely lost. That shit hurts… but it also lights a fire. It reminds me exactly why I’m not going back.

The key is harnessing it right. If you let it run wild, it turns into rage that wrecks everything. But if you channel it — like I did in wrestling when I turned being pinned into a reversal — it becomes controlled energy that protects your internal drive instead of burning you out.

Here’s what actually worked for me across all the rebuilds:

  • Reconnect to your real “why” — Dig past the surface. What kind of man do I want to be? What does a life I can be proud of look like? Then use the recycled anger to defend that vision.
  • Start stupidly small — On days the negative emotions are loud, I commit to just showing up and doing one hour of honest work. The anger often carries me into the full shift once competence starts kicking in.
  • Treat setbacks like data, not proof you’re broken — Relapse, lost job, bad day on the roof — I ask: “What need wasn’t being met here?” Then I adjust, and sometimes I let the anger at the setback fuel the fix.
  • Build routine and discipline on your own terms — Prison gave me structure; recovery taught me to own it. My morning routine includes that deliberate rumination time when needed — turning yesterday’s pain into today’s drive.
  • Celebrate the process more than the outcome — The real wins are the moments I harnessed the negativity instead of letting it harness me. “I felt like garbage but I still nailed that row of shingles and showed up for my family.”

If you’re in recovery, supporting someone, or just starting over with almost nothing, know this: the external motivators will come and go. The internal ones you build — including the smart way you recycle your own darkness — get stronger every rebuild.

You don’t need to feel motivated to start. You just need to start… and let the doing (and sometimes the righteous anger) rebuild the motivation.

I’m still a roofer in Ottawa. I still have hard days where I have to pull out the old wrestling mindset and remember how bad it used to be. But the drive now comes mostly from inside, and that’s what lets me keep going no matter what gets thrown at me next.

If this resonates and you’re trying to rebuild right now, reach out. I’ve been exactly where you are.
