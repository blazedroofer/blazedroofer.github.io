Lived Experience

Two Decades of Detox — Here's What I Actually Learned

I've been officially trying to fix my thought process since 2009. I don't have a number for relapses. What I have is a clear memory of what each one cost me — and what it taught me.

Josh Pearsall April 2026  ·  Recovery Roofer

I remember when I first started going into rehabs and treatment, they'd say relapse is part of the process. I honestly hated that saying. My body would tense up — I'm not sure if it was because they were in a position of authority, or if I just like to challenge things.

Playing sports my whole life, I understood commitment, how to work within a system, how to play your part. But that saying also made me question whether recovery was even possible. I have distinct memories from early on trying to figure out what it meant — what they meant by it.

The younger, more defensive version of me kind of enjoyed using it as an excuse. And I think part of me despised that side of myself.

I don't have a number for you. I think relapse is a unique experience, but there are some commonalities in why it happens and how we respond to it. I've been officially trying to "rebuild" or "fix my thought process" since 2009.

My first detox

The first detox I ever had, I didn't even know I was dependent. I had never been sick before. I had just returned to Acadia University in Wolfville, Nova Scotia. I couldn't sleep. I had anxiety — which up to that point I had never really experienced, beyond the normal acute stress a young person feels: do my friends like me, am I going to embarrass myself.

I was detoxing off oxys. I went to a student walk-in clinic and made up a story about back spasms. I had never experienced restless legs until that point in my life.

It lasted around five days, but ultimately it passed. I got busy with football and school, and I told myself over the Christmas break: that wasn't so bad. I'll just stop snorting and pop them on weekends. Forever the deal-maker with myself — the start of pushing the line on my own ethics, morals, and self-respect for the next decade or two.

My first jail detox

The first time I experienced withdrawal in jail was Canada Day 2011. I was hiding in my old childhood room, warrants out, my parents gone, and the cops rolling in.

I tried to hide above the furnace. They caught me, handcuffed me, and transported me to the Guelph police precinct.

I remember being scared. I also remember thinking: if I just talk to them and explain I'm a good guy, that this is some kind of misunderstanding, we can work this out. Looking back, I also remember thinking — how am I going to get dope in here? I actually considered having a friend pretend to drop off medication somehow, even though I had no prescriptions. What kind of person even thinks that? I didn't end up asking, but I seriously considered it.

Pretty quickly it hit me: the joke was over, I wanted to go home. Then I got angry and escalated — yelling, crying, acting like a toddler, throwing myself against the plexiglass. I even pretended to rip my underwear and tried to fake a hanging. Just wild thoughts.

I'm not sure anyone can fully explain what it feels like to lose your freedom for the first time. I didn't understand how the criminal justice machine worked. I didn't understand that once that process starts, it sees itself through regardless of how committed you are to it.

I was eventually transferred to Maplehurst. I was really sick over a few days. I didn't understand prison rules. I was a wrestler, so I figured I could handle myself if it came to that.

At one point I used a phone that belonged to someone else on the range. Someone told me it wasn't mine to use. I didn't really care.

Then I saw the guy coming down the stairs overlooking the range. He was practically running at me. I could see what was about to happen, and when he got close I hit him with the phone.

The next thing I knew I was dragged into the shower and beaten up.

I didn't understand the rules yet. After that, things changed. I stayed on the range, asked what the rules were, and learned them quickly. That was a lesson I never forgot.

Jail detoxes saved my life. There are immediate consequences, and I found they helped me turn my brain off — stop the constant scheming for another hit.

I learned there's another set of rules. I learned that if I went to jail, I would get clean before I got out.

I ended up in a rehab in Toronto, transported directly from Milton. That lasted 48 hours. Within a week I was homeless in Toronto.

My first withdrawal management detox

I found withdrawal management detox harder than jail detox — that's my unpopular opinion. I end up in the staff room saying I'm calling my dad, then I beg him for money, call him repeatedly while he's at work, escalate, and leave. I haven't had much success in detoxes.

I did successfully quit in a detox twice. The first was in 2012 when I first got to Ottawa. I went from detox to a sobriety house. During that process my world opened up to street life — detoxes and shelters that would be a big part of my life whenever I wasn't in custody or healthy and roofing.

Some people might say less structure is better. I'd agree with you when I'm using. But as I get sober, I realize how messed up my brain actually was. If we can't consent to bodily harm in Canada, I feel like the new drug supply really skirts the line of consent — it messes with your executive functioning so badly that you can't advocate for yourself or protect yourself.

The second time I successfully quit in a detox was probably my most successful comeback, or at least the one I'm most proud of. I ended up in Owen Sound, Ontario — coming from Ottawa, really sick off fentanyl and benzos. I was on methadone. I ended up quitting all of it. I was in detox for a month. I had a very minor episode of psychosis — sleep deprivation mostly — but it opened my eyes to what these drugs can actually do to your perception and your grip on reality. When that goes, how can you make real choices? You could hurt yourself or someone else. It taught me that even someone with no pre-existing mental health markers can cross over into delirium and psychosis.

My worst experience and what I learned

My worst detox was my last. I've suffered worse consequences in my life — I've served federal time. But the amount of damage I did to my brain in 2023, in such a short period of time, was something else.

I'll paint the picture briefly. I had a seizure quitting dope in Deep River. I should have been hospitalized. I refused treatment and ended up locked in a cell, incontinent.

I was released, went back to Ottawa, started roofing. Things improved. Then I had another seizure after using for a couple of weeks.

I was on a big flat roof. I recognized the warning signs. I walked to the drain in the middle before I lost consciousness.

The next thing I remember was a paramedic behind me and one in front of me, climbing down a ladder. I came to halfway down.

That was luck. If it had happened closer to the edge, the outcome would have been different. I refused treatment again, went home, and drank.

Things got slightly better, then in the fall I ended up in provincial jail with a very complicated withdrawal — seizure, delirium, emergency transfer to hospital, shackled to a gurney with two guards watching over me. I don't want to get into specifics. I've requested the FOI from that time. I'm still curious about exactly what went down.

What I learned: be honest with healthcare. For some reason I never knew how to advocate for myself, or I believed they wouldn't help me, or I'd be judged. I let things escalate into dangerous situations regarding my brain — because at some point you know you're not thinking straight, and that's terrifying. You don't know what's real.

I'm extremely lucky to still have my cognitive health. My freedom of choice. The ability to make plans and have a life. I was always told to protect my body, be strong. In 2023, I learned how scary delirium and seizures really are — and how they're processed in both the criminal justice system and healthcare.

This isn't a glorious account. There are many more stories. These are just some of the memorable ones where I learned something important.

The biggest thing I learned: be honest. It's not easy. I thought no one cared. I believed I'd be judged. The drug supply right now is nothing like we've ever seen — and it's my belief that we're going to see a significant spike in delirium and seizures over the next few years, as unregulated benzos and xylazine become more common in the supply.

Withdrawal used to mean a few miserable days. With the modern drug supply, it can mean seizures, delirium, and a hospital bed — if you're lucky enough to make it there.
