Life with a Record in Canada: The Cycle, the Fear, and the Long Way Back

From Guelph to federal time to Ottawa — and what perspective actually means after prison.

Josh Pearsall MONTH YEAR  ·  Recovery Roofer

Someone I really respect taught me this: he knows he's thinking poorly when he catches himself policing the grocery store line. Who's cutting. Who got there first. Why that person thinks they can just walk in and... and suddenly he's hypervigilant, tense, ready for a confrontation that exists entirely in his head. The guy in front of him is just buying milk.

That's the signal. Not the grocery store. Not the other people. The signal is the policing itself, the scanning, the ranking, the defensive crouch that kicks in when your nervous system hasn't fully learned yet that you're not inside anymore.

I got my first charge at 18. I've spent a long time learning to read that signal in myself. This is what I know.

How the Record Actually Built Itself

My record didn't come from one bad night. It came from a progression, and that progression has a logic worth being honest about.

The first charge was assault causing bodily harm. I was 18, just finished high school, drinking with friends, not even doing drugs at that point. A fight broke out with a guy from another school. That was it. No weapon, no plan, just a stupid escalating moment that got out of hand the way they do at that age. I got charged.

The second charge was the same, assault causing bodily harm, but the circumstances had shifted. I was in first year university, on the football team, at a rookie party. I was on ecstasy, blacked out, and ended up in an altercation I have only pieces of. That one told me something was changing, even if I wasn't ready to hear it. The substances were escalating. The situations they put me in were escalating with them.

Then football ended. I quit school. The structure that had held the shape of my life together, the team, the schedule, the identity of being an athlete, was gone, and nothing replaced it. I moved back to my parents' place in Guelph with a habit that needed feeding and no income to feed it. The charges that followed weren't fights anymore. They were theft. Robbery. Supporting myself the only way that felt available.

I stayed in Guelph like that until 2013. Then I moved to Ottawa. That was the first real decision I made that pointed somewhere different. Not recovery yet, not by a long stretch, but a direction that wasn't the same wall I kept hitting. Sometimes that's what a turning point looks like. Not a revelation. Just: I have to get out of this place.

Ottawa with a Record: The Wrong City for a Fresh Start

I moved to Ottawa thinking geography was enough. Get out of Guelph, get out of the environment, start over somewhere new. What I didn't understand yet was that I'd picked one of the hardest cities in Canada to land in with a criminal record.

Ottawa's economy runs on trust. The federal public service, defence contractors, cybersecurity firms, AI companies with government contracts, the entire ecosystem of consultants and staffing agencies that orbit them: almost all of it is built on security clearance. A record doesn't just close some doors here. It closes the dominant industry of the entire city. The federal government accounts for roughly a quarter of Ottawa's GDP, and the ripple effect is wider than that number suggests. Even private sector jobs that don't technically require clearance often screen as if they do, because their clients do. The pattern-matching goes deep.

That's the ceiling. Then there's the floor.

The jobs that have historically absorbed people with records, warehousing, logistics, entry-level trades support, food service, general labour, are exactly the ones being compressed right now by automation and AI. The work that didn't ask questions is disappearing faster than anything else in the economy. Nationally, unemployment is shifting in ways that hit the bottom of the labour market hardest, and people with records sit at the bottom of the bottom. They have the least cushion and the fewest alternatives when a category of work dries up.

And Ottawa's cost of living has tracked upward with government expansion for years. The city is priced for people with government salaries or tech sector incomes. If you arrived here with a record, limited credentials, and a history that closes most formal employment pipelines, you're being asked to survive in a city that wasn't built with any margin for you.

I felt that gap immediately. The move to Ottawa was the right instinct. But arriving here clarified something I hadn't fully reckoned with: leaving the environment wasn't enough if the new environment had its own version of the same walls, just cleaner and quieter. Nobody was going to say your record was the problem. The application just wouldn't go anywhere. The callback wouldn't come.

That's not unique to Ottawa, but it's sharper here than most places. And as AI continues compressing the jobs that don't ask for clearances or credentials, it's going to get sharper everywhere.

What a Record Does to Your Options

By the time I understood the full shape of my record, the walls it built were already standing.

Security clearances were gone. A lot of entry-level jobs were gone. The straightforward paths most people my age were walking, I couldn't get on them. So I drifted into construction and roofing, sectors that often need bodies willing to grind and don't always run thorough checks. That wasn't a plan. It was what was left.

What I didn't see then was that the same environment giving me work was also normalizing the substances that helped build the record in the first place. And the cycle kept running: use, trouble, charges, fewer options, more stress, more use.

A criminal record doesn't just follow you. It narrows the field in ways that push you back toward the same conditions that created the problem. More stress from instability deepens the need to cope. Coping leads to more trouble. Each breach reset the clock, added another layer, made the record longer and harder to explain. I knew that cycle from the inside. I just didn't have the language for it yet.

The Fear That Keeps You Silent

The grocery store hypervigilance doesn't stay in grocery stores. It follows you into job sites, parole offices, any room where you already feel like you're at a disadvantage before anyone's said a word.

For me that fear showed up as silence. When I was struggling, the last thing I wanted was to raise my hand. Bringing a problem to anyone official felt like handing them another reason to see me as unreliable, another mark, another consequence. So I stayed quiet and let things fester until they couldn't anymore. Missed meetings. Failed tests. Things that might have been manageable under different circumstances became breaches because I was too afraid to say something early.

Federal time hardened that instinct into something deeper. The code inside is simple: you never rat. Talking to guards or officials about anything could get you labelled fast. That mindset followed me out. With a record already against me I felt permanently marked, like everyone in any room could see exactly what I was.

The thing is, that feeling is real. It's just not accurate. Prison has its own rules, its own subcultures, its own moral code. Short term that's understandable. Long term those lines get blurry. Then they disappear. Coming out means redrawing them for yourself: what's an actual threat, what's just someone in a checkout line, what's the grocery store policing and what's a real problem worth addressing.

Ratting vs. Reporting: Getting That Straight

The shift that changed how I operated most practically was finally separating ratting from reporting, because I'd been treating them as the same thing, and it was costing me.

Ratting is using someone else's vulnerability for personal gain. Snitching on someone's hustle to benefit yourself, to cut time, to get ahead at someone else's expense. Inside, that code exists for real reasons.

Reporting is something else entirely. It's protecting yourself, your family, your stability when something genuinely threatens them. It has nothing to do with anyone else's business. It's refusing to let your own situation fall apart because you were too afraid to say something out loud.

Once I got that distinction clear I started erring hard toward compliance. I follow the rules now, even the small ones. I flag problems early instead of letting them become breaches. The old strategy, stay quiet, avoid conflict, hope it resolves itself, is exactly what kept landing me back in trouble. I already have more to lose than most people in the room. Silence has never once made that better.

Record Suspension: What It Does and What It Doesn't

A record suspension, formerly called a pardon, is an order from the Parole Board of Canada that keeps your criminal record separate from other records. Once granted, it's removed from most standard employment and CPIC background checks. It doesn't erase the conviction, but it removes the automatic red flag that follows you into job applications, housing, and anything else that runs a check.

To be eligible you have to complete your full sentence, parole, probation, all fines, then wait five years for summary offences or ten years for indictable ones, with no new convictions in that window. Processing takes six to eighteen months and involves gathering court documents and fees. Some people aren't eligible depending on the nature or number of their convictions.

For many people it's worth pursuing. It opens more doors in trades, can trigger human rights protections against discrimination when the offence is unrelated to the job, and removes the constant weight of carrying something that never goes away. Only about three percent of people who receive one ever have it revoked, because they go on to stay out of trouble.

But it doesn't make bias disappear. Employers can still discriminate informally, especially with a long or complicated record. Years of charges don't clean up neatly regardless of what the paperwork says. I want to be honest about this because there's a version of this conversation that strings people along, that talks up lived experience and recovery while quietly keeping real advancement just out of reach. Steady employment is one of the strongest protections against relapse. When records keep closing doors, people cycle back into the same environments. Fair-chance hiring and real access to suspensions aren't charity. They're practical.

Perspective Is the Work

The Ottawa move in 2013 wasn't recovery. It was just a different direction, away from the specific place where the same patterns kept repeating. That matters. Sometimes the first step isn't insight. It's just geography, a decision to stop being somewhere that isn't working.

What came after that, the slower work of learning to read my own signals, to tell the difference between a real threat and a grocery store line, to report instead of going quiet, to flag the problem before it becomes a breach, that took a lot longer. It still does.

My respect for the person who gave me the grocery store example comes partly from the fact that he wasn't offering a cure. He was offering a signal, something to watch for in yourself so you can catch the distorted thinking before it runs the show.

How you see yourself. How you see others. How you play a role in both. That's the work. It doesn't finish. But it has a direction.
