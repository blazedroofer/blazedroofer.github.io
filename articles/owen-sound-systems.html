System & Policy

When Small Systems Work: What Owen Sound Taught Me About Addiction Recovery

I remember being on the 401 with a man I barely knew, running out of gas, walking for help with nothing but desperation. That moment led me to Owen Sound — not because it was perfect, but because for a small community it made recovery easier to enter and easier to navigate.

Josh Pearsall · April 2026 · 9 min read
Here's what matters most
  • Smaller systems can reduce friction — fewer layers, clearer next steps, easier to navigate when you're in crisis.
  • A good enough next step right now is often better than a perfect step later. Momentum matters.
  • Front-line workers who bridge gaps and push for placements are what often make systems actually work.
  • Recovery rarely follows a perfect linear path, but lower-friction entry points help people keep moving forward.

The Ride That Changed Direction

I remember being on the 401 with a man I barely knew. I needed a ride from Ottawa to Guelph so I could get out of the situation I was in. The plan was built more on desperation than common sense.

Then we ran out of gas. We had no money, no backup plan. I ended up walking down the 401, trying to solve a problem that should never have existed. Eventually I traded my old Chatr phone for gas and a jerry can.

Somehow, I got home. My parents were away. When they came back we had one of those conversations that cuts through denial. I thought about my son. I knew I needed help and some distance from my environment.

I remembered hearing someone I knew had found success in Owen Sound. My dad made the two-hour drive, and that is how Owen Sound entered my life.

The Question That Matters Most

A lot of public conversation about addiction gets stuck in ideology — abstinence versus harm reduction, treatment versus enforcement. Those debates matter, but they often miss the practical question that matters most to a person in crisis: when you finally decide you want help, what happens next?

That is where Owen Sound stood out to me.

What a Continuum of Care Actually Means

The best way to explain a continuum of care is this: one kind of help leads to the next kind of help without a long, confusing gap. Someone may need detox, then treatment, then housing, then ongoing support. The point is that they connect.

You can think of it like stepping stones across a river. If the stones are close together, you keep moving. If the gaps are too wide, you fall in.

Recovery often depends on momentum. Delay has a cost that does not always show up in a waitlist number.

The People Who Hold the System Together

Before I say anything else about systems, I want to say something about the people inside them. I do not think I would still be alive without front-line workers — peer workers, nurses, doctors, counselors, even some correctional guards and parole officers.

Most detoxes are reasonably accessible if you keep calling and staff can see you are serious. Front-line workers move mountains for people who are showing real motivation. That is often everything.

What Owen Sound Got Right: Reducing Friction

What stood out to me in Owen Sound was not that it was perfect. It was the lack of friction. In a smaller place, the system felt easier to understand. The next step felt clearer. There were fewer moving parts and less sense of being lost in a maze.

Ottawa has a lot going for it, but that can also make things confusing and overwhelming. More options do not always mean easier access, especially for someone in crisis. Sometimes small communities reduce friction simply by being more navigable.

My Own Path Was Not Perfectly Linear

I did a referral to a recovery house in Owen Sound but did not end up staying there long-term. I stayed in detox for about a month, then returned to Ottawa and completed a 90-day program, followed by second-stage housing. Recovery is rarely linear.

But Owen Sound still matters in my story — and in the stories of people close to me — because it offered a powerful starting point. Some of those people built stable lives afterward. That is no small thing.

The Bigger Lesson

This is not about saying one city cares and another does not. It is about asking a simpler question: what is actually working, and why?

In this case, what seemed to work was a lower-friction system. Easier entry. Clearer transitions. Less confusion. A path that felt real.

Treatment alone is not enough, and housing alone is not enough either. A person can leave treatment and get placed somewhere that looks stable on paper but still not have the life skills or support needed to manage it.

Owen Sound, at least in my experience, seemed better than most at closing some of those early gaps. For a small community, that is no small thing.
