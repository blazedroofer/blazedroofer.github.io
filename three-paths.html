Three Paths After Treatment: What I Would Actually Do

After detox or stabilization, the next step depends on your situation. Here’s how I would approach it in each scenario:

1. Starting from Zero (Homeless & in Active Addiction)

I’d treat this like a full-court press.

First priority: Get into detox. I’d call Ottawa Withdrawal Management Services every single day until I got a bed. If Ottawa was full, I’d start calling Kingston next.

While still in detox, I’d push for an OAARS assessment. That opens doors to Serenity House, Sobriety House, Jericho Road, Life House, Anchorage, or Brock Cottage.

If I couldn’t get a rehab bed right away, I’d sign up for the Shelter Stabilization / Day Program at the Ottawa Mission or Salvation Army. These programs have upgraded facilities, fewer people per room, lockers you don’t have to move every day, and structured daytime programming.

From there, I’d keep momentum going by attending Life House or Anchorage programs while waiting for my chosen rehab bed.

Important note: If you go the shelter stabilization route, you usually don’t need an OAARS assessment first because many of those programs are city-funded.

2. Starting with a Job (Especially Union Job)

This path is much smoother if you have a job and benefits.

I’d start by talking to my union rep right away and accessing Short-Term Disability (STD) or Long-Term Disability (LTD) benefits.

With benefits in place, I’d have way more options — including private treatment centers that often have shorter or no waitlists and private rooms if you want to pay extra.

Many of the places I listed above also have a private-pay option, which can get you in faster.

The biggest advantage: you stay connected to your job and union. You have an active return-to-work plan, aftercare support, and a clear path back to independence.

3. Starting in the Criminal Justice System

If you’re in the criminal justice system, ask your lawyer about Drug Treatment Court (DTC).

DTC is only available through the court system and they don’t accept everyone.

You have to follow your lawyer’s guidance closely on this.

If accepted, it’s a structured program that combines treatment, accountability, and court supervision. It can be a real lifeline if you qualify.

Final Thought

No matter which path you’re on, the key is the same: get stable first, then move into the right level of care. Don’t wait for perfect conditions — start where you are and keep momentum going.

The systems are far from perfect, but they can work if you stay persistent and strategic.
