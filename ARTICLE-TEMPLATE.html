Inside the 72-Hour Window: What Actually Happens Once the Door Closes

What the Act authorizes between apprehension and the hearing — the assessment report, the right to refuse, the authority to use force — and why the safeguards are built for a person who isn't there.

By Josh Pearsall · Filed Apr 2026 · 12 min read · Ottawa

The 72 hours start the moment the door closes. Not when you're told what's happening. Not when you sign anything. The clock starts when someone with statutory authority decides it has, and from that point forward almost everything written into the Act assumes a version of you that does not currently exist — clear-headed, capable of reading a form, capable of refusing a thing you don't yet know is being done.

That gap is the whole story. It's also the part nobody is going to put in the press release.

What the Act actually authorizes

The plain text of Section 17(3) is short. The implications aren't.

The director shall ensure that an assessment is completed within 72 hours of apprehension. — Compassionate Intervention Act, s. 17(3)

Here is what that sentence does not say. It does not say who conducts the assessment, what their training is, what they can ask, what they can record, what happens to the recording. It does not say what counts as a refusal. It does not say what happens when the person being assessed is in active withdrawal and physically cannot answer the questions in a way the form will accept.

The right to refuse, in theory

Subsection (4) gives the apprehended person a right to refuse the assessment. On paper that sounds like a meaningful safeguard. As I covered in Part 3, who actually gets to start this process is broader than people assume — and a right that depends on you being able to read, understand, and articulate it inside 72 hours of forced sobriety is a right designed for someone who doesn't need it.

The first time I went through detox in Ottawa, I could not have told you my own birthday on day two. Not because I didn't know it. Because the part of my brain that retrieves it had stopped showing up for work.

The authority to use force

This is the section nobody quotes in the press release, and the one that matters most. The Act authorizes "such force as is reasonably necessary" — language borrowed from Mental Health Act precedent — but extends it to a population the Mental Health Act was never designed for.

The legal phrase looks reasonable until you stand inside it.
