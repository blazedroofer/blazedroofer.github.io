My Story

I thought I was different.
I wasn't.

My life has moved between two very different worlds. One included university football, steady work, and the kind of opportunities people assume lead somewhere good. The other included addiction, homelessness, and federal prison. For a long time I moved back and forth between both.

Josh Pearsall Recovery Roofer · Ottawa · Updated 2024

The Touchdown

"Quads right. Slice 9129."

Keith calls it in the huddle. We break. I'm the second receiver. Pre-snap I see it — SAM linebacker's creeping up, he's blitzing. Ball snaps. He comes. I cut my slant high, ball hits me in stride. I plant, explode upfield. The safeties split for a second and I'm gone.

First career touchdown. University of Montreal, second year. The crowd going crazy, my boys mobbing me. This was it. This was who I was. I scored almost every game after that — finished the season leading the team in touchdowns.

I thought I had it all figured out. Thought I was untouchable. Thought I was different.

I'd headed to Acadia University from Ontario thinking I could outrun my problems. New environment, ditch the sketchy friends — fresh start. If I got far enough away, things would be different.

Wrong. I couldn't outrun myself. The common denominator in all my messes was me.

The First Cracks

During breaks when I returned to Ontario I began experimenting with opioids. At first it seemed manageable. I'd use over Christmas or in the summer and then return to school and stop. That illusion didn't last long.

The real problem was that every time I went back to Guelph for holidays, I dove right back into heavier use — then detoxed cold turkey when I returned to campus. A vicious cycle I ignored because the field was my escape. As long as football was working, everything else got swept under the rug.

In my second month as a freshman, after a rookie party, things escalated fast. I ended up with an assault charge — my first real bid. 30 days on weekends at Mimico Detention Center.

I remember sitting with two other guys from London. They were scheming about stealing a TV. I piped up, told them how stupid it sounded. They just laughed. They'd already pegged me as the fish who didn't get it. In my head I was screaming — don't these guys know I'm different? I play football. I'm in university. I have potential. I was offended as hell, but I didn't show it.

I kept playing. I kept using when I was home. I kept telling myself it was manageable.

Thanksgiving 2010 — The End of Football

Thanksgiving weekend, third year. Supposed to be my breakout season. I borrowed a hundred bucks, drove my roommate to the airport, hit the casino. Won five hundred dollars. Right there I made the decision — we're flying back to Guelph for the weekend. I'd be back for practice.

I never went back.

A few weeks later my parents figured out I wasn't returning to school. They told me I couldn't stay. Bought me a bus ticket to Ottawa. Sent me to rehab.

Winter in Ottawa. I stepped off that bus with a hockey bag full of clothes and forty dollars. No phone. I had to ask someone where the shelter was.

I checked into detox. Then rehab. And I actually completed the program. Graduation day came and I was supposed to go to sober living, start fresh, do it right.

Instead I met up with Corey, Matt, and Joel — guys from the group ahead of me, already in second stage. They were using. So I used with them.

Within two years, all three of them were dead. I'm the only one left.

Roofing, Fatherhood, and the Fentanyl Patches

After leaving rehab I caught a break — got a roofing job. My dad helped me get an apartment. Sixty days in Ottawa and I had a place, income, a future that looked like something.

Then I met a girl. She got pregnant. I was going to be a dad.

I thought I'd turned it around. I was still using — smoking fentanyl patches — but I was working, selling roofs, bringing in money. Then one day she came downstairs, pregnant, and caught me bent over tinfoil with a straw. I tried to tell her it was weed.

She wasn't buying it. I left. Told myself it was because of my record. That was a lie I told myself. I left because the drugs mattered more.

Jack was born premature at 32 weeks. His mom called me — let's go to the hospital. I thought it was too early, that she was overreacting. By the time she got there she was dilated, transferred to CHEO. Jack was born within an hour. My parents drove down. I went to work.

Things were okay for a bit after that. A baby is a good distraction. Then the baby bonus came — six thousand dollars. Money for diapers, formula, everything a premature baby needs.

I spent it all in weeks. On dope.

We got evicted. She and Jack moved in with my parents. I wasn't allowed to come. I ended up in a shelter.

The Elvis Judge

That 2011 charge should have been a theft. I'd stolen someone's prescription — a guy who used to sell me pills. But I got charged with robbery. I was sick, mad at my parents for not bailing me out, and I said fuck it, I'll plead guilty. My lawyer Rhanny tried to stop me. I went through with it anyway.

The judge asked me what happened. I told the truth. He struck my guilty plea on the spot — said it sounded like a theft, not a robbery, and made the Crown take it back.

That judge was Douglas. The papers called him Stormin' Norman. Apparently he impersonated Elvis to relax. The Guelph Mercury ran a piece on him around that time — it mentioned he'd recently struck a guilty plea from a man addicted to OxyContin, telling him "so much is at stake for you that you would be nuts to plead guilty to robbery today."

That man was me. I didn't know enough to be grateful at the time.

2016 — Owen Sound and Back

In 2016 I got clean again. A guy from Guelph picked me up, drove me to Owen Sound for detox. Then a 90-day program in Ottawa. Finished it. Got my own place. Eight months later I relapsed.

What followed was the worst stretch. 2018 into 2019. On the streets, injecting, doing whatever I had to do to get the next hit.

Rock Bottom

About three weeks before I went to prison, I went to help a kid named Booster Phil. A drug dealer had chained him to a fence. I knew everyone involved and I needed dope. I had fifty bucks my dad had given me.

I walked toward the safe injection site and they rushed me. Started beating me. One of them spotted the bill clenched in my fist.

"He's got a 50!"

I shoved that bill up my ass. They kept beating me — broke my nose, stole my shoes — but I didn't let go of the money. I walked into the safe injection site, went to the bathroom, fished it out so I could go cop.

A guy I knew from AA walked me out to make sure I didn't get jumped again. He asked me: "Don't you think this is rock bottom?"

I just wanted to get to the other safe injection site so I could use.

May 6th, 2019

I woke up sick. I needed money. I took a cab to Shoppers, stole a bunch of stuff. There was a commotion. I panicked, jumped in the back of someone's car and took it. High-speed chase.

I got high afterward. On Monday I turned myself in. Pled guilty. Got 48 months.

Jail became my detox. I'd learned to love it in a twisted way. I needed to stop and I knew I was in a lot of trouble. I started at OCDC, sick as hell. Didn't ask for bail. I pled out, took the 48-month joint submission. Got transferred to Joyceville. Beaver Creek became my home institution.

Parole, Relapse, and a More Dangerous Supply

I was paroled in August 2020. Finished with no violations, no problems. Then two months before parole ended, I started using again.

By 2023 it was almost as bad as before — except faster. The drug supply had changed. Unregulated benzos, xylazine mixed into everything, unpredictable doses. I started having seizures. Withdrawal was more intense than anything I'd experienced before.

I was roofing with my brother, using every day. I tried to quit and the seizures got worse. Eventually I caught another charge — six months.

March 2024

When I was released in March 2024, something was different. I didn't try to rebuild everything at once. I focused on three things: show up to work, maintain recovery, build stability one day at a time.

I returned to roofing. I haven't missed a day or been late in over a year.

I didn't learn it in rehab. I didn't learn it in prison. I learned it by showing up. And I didn't stop throwing punches.

What I Know Now

You can't outrun yourself. I went from Ontario to Nova Scotia thinking a change of scenery would fix me. The common denominator in all my messes was me.

Rock bottom is a choice. I had a hundred rock bottoms. Shoving money up my ass with a broken nose wasn't enough. Stealing my son's baby bonus wasn't enough. The carjacking wasn't enough. You don't hit bottom because things get bad enough — you hit bottom when you decide you're done.

Recovery isn't about being tough. I spent years thinking I just needed to work harder, push through. I was already living life in fifth gear. What I needed was to learn how to exist in first or second. To sit with uncomfortable feelings instead of running from them.

If you're reading this and you think you're different — you're not. And that's actually good news. Because if I can claw my way out, so can you.

Why This Site Exists

I'm not a counsellor or a medical professional. I'm someone who has been through the cycle of addiction, treatment, relapse, incarceration, and rebuilding more times than I'd like to count.

What I can offer is honesty about what that looks like — and practical insight into navigating recovery in Ottawa. If you're struggling or watching someone you care about struggle, reach out. Or start with the continuum of care page to understand what options actually exist.
