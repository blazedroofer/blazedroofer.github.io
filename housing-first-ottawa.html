Housing First in Ottawa: It Sounds Good, But Here's What I Have Seen From the Inside

Housing is important. Everyone needs a safe place to start rebuilding. But after years in the system, rehabs, jails, shelters, and the streets, I have learned that just giving someone keys without the right supports often sets them up to fail. Here is my perspective from lived experience in Ottawa.

I grew up with prosocial parents who always showed up. At the start of my addiction, they got me into places, paid for rehabs, and believed the big promises from programs. They poured in love, money, and hope. I had good intentions too. But I never learned basic life skills: cleaning, hygiene, budgeting, cooking, keeping a place together. Everything was provided in closed systems: structured rehabs, jail, shelters. When I finally got housing, even trying hard, I struggled. Things broke, messes built up, money got mismanaged. It felt like wasting the very resources meant to help.

Today in Ottawa, Housing First is still the main model. The City of Ottawa Housing Services Branch runs it through a Coordinated Access system. They partner with nonprofits like CMHA Ottawa, Options Bytown, The Ottawa Mission, Salvation Army, and Indigenous organizations such as Minwaashin Lodge, Wabano, and Tungasuvvingat Inuit. The idea is simple: get people into permanent housing fast, without preconditions like sobriety or treatment first. Supports like case management come after.

In theory, that stability helps. In practice, especially with worse drugs like fentanyl, it creates problems. Crises such as overdoses, open dealing, property damage, and safety issues often get dumped onto Ottawa Police, nurses in the ER, outreach workers, and the community. Frontline folks are burned out. Neighbours deal with the fallout in higher density buildings. It can feel contagious, and the power imbalance is real. Dealers target housed addicts.

The system still feels reactive to me. Everything is spread out: different agencies, waitlists, scattered supports. Steps get missed. There is no strong continuum of care from medical detox and stabilization to structured support to learning life skills to independent living.

Right now, Ontario is making a big shift. The province is ending funding for supervised consumption sites in Ottawa. The two remaining ones at Sandy Hill and Ottawa Inner City Health are closing in June 2026. They are pushing HART Hubs (Homelessness and Addiction Recovery Treatment Hubs) instead: recovery focused places with primary care, mental health, substance use supports, and some housing navigation. Two are operating in Ottawa already (Pinecrest Queensway and the one on Eccles Street). The goal is more treatment and less just enabling use.

I think this direction makes sense, but it needs to go further. We should consolidate resources and build a real pathway.

Start with accessible medical detox and withdrawal management so people are not operating in primal survival mode. My brain in withdrawal felt like IQ 0: pure instinct to find relief until my body snapped back.

Build in life skills training: hygiene, cleaning, budgeting, routines. Most of us never learned them coming from shelters or the street.

Add real guard rails and accountability so housing does not get ruined and communities are not hurt more.

Encourage contribution once stabilized: labour crews for cleanup, maintenance, needle pickup. Needles on the ground suck, and hepatitis C treatment is expensive. People sick with open wounds cannot think straight, let alone advocate for help or do basic hygiene.

When you are that deep in it, you cannot just choose recovery or manage an apartment. My parents efforts taught me that love and resources alone are not enough without the tools and structure.

Housing First has good intentions and has housed people, but without a stronger medical and skills based foundation, we keep cycling. The HART Hubs shift is a step toward recovery over pure harm reduction. Let us make sure the transition actually builds that full continuum so no one falls through the cracks.

What do folks on the front lines or with lived experience think? How do we make supports actually stick in Ottawa?
